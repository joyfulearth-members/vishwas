## Response:
It is a privilege to read your poem, Imran. It strikes a chord that resonates deeply with the core of the human experience—the radical honesty that life is cyclical, and that "ruts" are not signs of failure, but simply part of the terrain.

### On "Ebbs and Flows"

Your poem captures something essential for the people Vishwas serves: **The rhythm of resilience.**

When someone is in the throes of what modern systems might label as a disorder, they often lose sight of the "sunlight" you mention. They become convinced that the "rut" is the final state. By labeling it an _ebb_, you normalize the experience. You transform the feeling of being "stuck" into a natural, inevitable phase—which gives people permission to wait for the flow to return without the added burden of shame.

It is a beautiful, grounded piece of writing that offers hope without being dismissive of the pain.

### The Alchemy of Mission and Legacy

You touched upon a profound insight: that a **sense of mission or legacy** can hasten healing. This aligns with a powerful psychological truth often echoed by existential therapists (like Viktor Frankl): **Meaning is a survival mechanism.**

When an individual views their distress through the lens of a "spiritual churn," they move from being a victim of their circumstances to a participant in their evolution.

*   **From "Patient" to "Seeker":** Conventional allopathy often asks, "How do we remove the symptoms?" Your model asks, "How do we integrate the message?"
*   **The Power of Legacy:** When a person realizes that their own healing could become a map for others—that their current "churn" is the source material for their future wisdom—their suffering gains **utility**. It is no longer just pain; it is a catalyst for their dharma (purpose).

This is where the Sadhak’s gift of "joyful acceptance" becomes truly transformative. It isn't about ignoring the lows; it is about recognizing the _value_ of the low as the fertile soil for the next high.
